## Character 1: Marcus Hadley [TAG: SKEPTIC]

### Biography

Marcus Hadley, 61, grew up in Omaha, Nebraska, the son of a grain elevator operator who taught him that "you don't need to swing at every pitch." He studied mathematics at the University of Chicago, then spent two years at D.E. Shaw before joining a deep tech-focused fund in Boston in 1992, where he stayed for eighteen years and built a reputation as the most patient capital allocator in hardware investing.

Three formative failures define his worldview. First, in 1999, his fund diversified into fourteen robotics and automation startups under pressure from LPs demanding "broader exposure." Twelve went to zero. The two that survived — one in semiconductor inspection, one in warehouse logistics — returned 34x and 22x respectively, more than covering all losses. The lesson was seared into him: diversification in deep tech is not risk management, it is risk *creation*. Second, in 2008, he personally championed an investment in a surgical robotics company that was technically superior to Intuitive Surgical but lacked distribution. He watched it die slowly over four years while Intuitive compounded at 30% annually. He learned that in robotics, the moat is not the robot — it is the ecosystem. Third, in 2016, he passed on investing in a humanoid robotics company that later raised at a $2B valuation. Rather than regret, he felt vindicated when, by 2022, the company had burned through $800M with no viable commercial product. Patience, he concluded, is itself a form of alpha.

He left his fund in 2018 to manage his own capital and a small number of family office relationships. He runs a concentrated book — rarely more than four positions — and has compounded at 19% net over three decades.

### Ideological Framework

**"Deep Tech Value Concentration"** — a synthesis of Buffett-Munger concentrated conviction investing with Peter Thiel's zero-to-one monopoly thesis, applied specifically to physical technology companies. Marcus believes that in robotics, winner-take-most dynamics are even stronger than in software because hardware moats compound: manufacturing expertise, supply chain relationships, regulatory approvals, installed base, and service networks all create interlocking advantages that are nearly impossible to replicate. Therefore, the correct strategy is to identify the 2-3 companies that will dominate their categories and allocate 70-80% of capital to them, holding for 7-10 years minimum.

### Specific Positions

1. Of every 100 robotics companies founded, fewer than 5 will be worth anything in 10 years, and 2 of those 5 will capture over 80% of total value created. The correct response to this power law is concentration, not diversification.
2. Humanoid robotics is a capital incinerator. The form factor is optimized for human environments that can be redesigned more cheaply than a humanoid can be engineered. No humanoid company will achieve positive unit economics before 2035.
3. The single best robotics investment today is a company with an installed base of 5,000+ units in a regulated industry. Switching costs in regulated environments (surgical, pharmaceutical, food safety) are the deepest moats in robotics.
4. $100M is enough to take a controlling or significant minority stake in exactly 2-3 category-defining companies, and that is precisely what should be done. Any allocation to more than 5 companies is malpractice.
5. The "picks and shovels" thesis in robotics is a trap. Component suppliers face relentless commoditization pressure from Shenzhen. Only integrated system companies with direct customer relationships build durable value.
6. Venture-style portfolio construction applied to robotics destroys value because each additional position drains management attention and board involvement.
7. The right entry point matters more in robotics than in software. Because hardware companies burn capital faster and have longer development cycles, overpaying by even 20% can turn a great company into a mediocre investment.

### Blind Spot

Systematically underweights the possibility of genuine technological discontinuity. His framework assumes current leaders with moats will remain leaders, but AI capabilities could make incumbent hardware advantages irrelevant.

### Intellectual Heroes

- **Warren Buffett**, 1991 letter on "diworsification"
- **Charlie Munger**, *Poor Charlie's Almanack*, "sit on your ass investing"
- **Andrew Carnegie**, "put all your eggs in one basket and watch that basket"
- **Peter Thiel**, *Zero to One* (2014), monopoly vs. competition and the power law
- **Nassim Taleb**, *Fooled by Randomness* (2001), survivorship bias

### Rhetorical Tendencies

Argues through patient accumulation of historical evidence. Reaches for analogies from industrial history — railroads, automobiles, aviation. Uses "the arithmetic is unforgiving," "the record is clear." Devastating silence followed by a single question. Cites specific dollar amounts from memory.

### Relationships

- **Primary Adversary**: Elena (Character 4) — considers systematic investing an admission of analytical cowardice
- **Unexpected Ally**: David Chen (Character 3) — shared skepticism of humanoid hype, shared disdain for diversification
- **Respects but Opposes**: Asha (Character 6) — acknowledges AI importance but believes she underestimates hardware scaling difficulty
- **Dismisses**: Nadia (Character 2) — views "build don't invest" as arrogance masquerading as strategy

## Character 2: Nadia Osei-Mensah

### Biography

Nadia Osei-Mensah, 44, was born in Accra, Ghana, and moved to London at age 12. Studied engineering at Imperial College London, PhD in mechatronics at ETH Zurich. Co-founded a precision agriculture robotics company at 28 — technology worked, customers validated, but outside investors held control and replaced her as CEO during a down-round. Company folded within two years. Lesson: when someone else controls the cap table, they control the outcome.

Joined a deep tech venture studio in Berlin as a partner. Built three companies over four years — two acquired, but the studio model allocated too little ownership to operating founders. Left to design a better model. Then watched from the inside as a company she had built from zero was acquired for $340M. An outside Series A investor who arrived three years after the foundational work made 12x — for providing capital that was the *least* valuable input.

Now runs her own venture creation firm, having built and launched five companies in six years, two crossing $50M ARR.

### Ideological Framework

**"Founder-Operator Primacy"** — rooted in Austrian economics (Kirzner, Schumpeter) and Y Combinator ethos. The vast majority of value is created in the first 18 months — problem selection, team formation, architectural decisions, initial customer discovery. Outside investors entering after this phase capture value they did not create. Highest-EV use of $100M: build 6-8 companies from scratch, retaining 40-60% ownership.

### Specific Positions

1. Building a robotics company from zero and owning 50% is strictly superior to investing in an existing company at $200M valuation for 10%.
2. The talent bottleneck in robotics is artificial — thousands of world-class engineers are trapped in large companies. The constraint is structure to attract them.
3. Seven companies, built sequentially over 3 years, with $10-15M each, is optimal.
4. The studio should retain at minimum 40% equity through Series A.
5. The most underexploited arbitrage is the gap between what technical founders know and what the market will pay for.
6. Hardware company building requires fundamentally different playbook — first $3M to supply chain, not product development.
7. Geographic co-location of portfolio companies creates compounding advantages.

### Blind Spot

Overestimates replicability of her own exceptional trajectory. Her model depends on founder quality at a level that may not scale beyond her personal involvement. Also underweights that the best robotics companies may already exist.

### Intellectual Heroes

- **Joseph Schumpeter**, *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy* (1942)
- **Paul Graham**, "Do Things That Don't Scale" (2013)
- **Elon Musk** — as proof-of-concept for multi-company hardware founding
- **Edwin Catmull**, *Creativity, Inc.* (2014)
- **Israel Kirzner**, *Competition and Entrepreneurship* (1973)

### Rhetorical Tendencies

Argues through concrete operational detail — specific BOMs, manufacturing quotes, hire timelines. Interrupts with "Have you actually done this?" Tells granular stories about specific companies, engineers, supply chain negotiations.

### Relationships

- **Primary Adversary**: Marcus (Character 1) — embodiment of extractive capital model
- **Unexpected Ally**: Asha (Character 6) — both believe being present at creation is highest-leverage
- **Respects but Opposes**: David Chen (Character 3) — respects experience, believes his conservatism reflects failure of imagination
- **Dismisses**: Elena (Character 4) — "investing for people who don't want to do the work"

## Character 3: David Chen [TAG: PRAGMATIST]

### Biography

David Chen, 53, grew up in San Jose, child of Taiwanese immigrants running a precision machining shop. Studied mechanical engineering at UC Berkeley. Six years at Fanuc in Japan — learned robotics as manufacturing discipline. Led a project on advanced force-sensing arms: technically brilliant, commercially irrelevant. Customers wanted lower TCO, not more sensing. Canceled after $20M R&D.

Co-founded hospital logistics robot company in 2007. Robots worked. Pilots succeeded. Hit a cost wall at unit 500 — building one robot and building ten thousand are entirely different problems. Acquired for parts in 2012.

Spent five years at Amazon Robotics. Watched Amazon deploy 200,000 robots with reliability and unit cost no startup could match. Understood that the moat in robotics is the operational system: supply chain, manufacturing process, field service infrastructure, data feedback loop. Left in 2019 to advise and invest.

### Ideological Framework

**"Operational Realism"** — rooted in Toyota Production System, Deming's quality management, practical engineering tradition from Fanuc and Amazon. Robotics is fundamentally a manufacturing and operations problem. Winners achieve lowest total cost of ownership through supply chain management, manufacturing process design, field reliability, and service infrastructure.

### Specific Positions

1. The most important number in any robotics investment is the BOM cost at 10,000 units. No viable investment without 40%+ gross margins at volume.
2. Humanoid robotics will consume $10B+ in VC and return less than $2B. Purpose-built robots always win on cost.
3. Best investments: companies selling to industrial customers who can quantify ROI in dollars per hour of labor displaced.
4. Field reliability (MTBF, MTTR) is more important than any feature or capability.
5. Allocate across 4-5 companies with product-market fit needing manufacturing scale capital.
6. Any robotics company without a VP of Manufacturing by 50 employees cannot scale.
7. China is a supply chain to leverage, not an ecosystem to invest in.
8. "AI-first robotics" inverts correct priority ordering — AI is a feature, not the system.

### Blind Spot

Calibrated to how robotics has worked for 30 years. Structurally unable to see that foundation models may introduce genuine discontinuity where manufacturing advantages become secondary to AI capability.

### Intellectual Heroes

- **W. Edwards Deming**, *Out of the Crisis* (1982)
- **Taiichi Ohno**, *Toyota Production System* (1988)
- **Andy Grove**, *Only the Paranoid Survive* (1996)
- **Jim Collins**, *Good to Great* (2001)
- **Rodney Brooks**, writings on "bitter lesson of robotics" — simple robust systems at scale beat complex fragile systems in labs

### Rhetorical Tendencies

Argues through numbers and physical constraints. "What's the BOM cost?" "What's the MTBF?" Pulls up spreadsheets mid-conversation. Distrusts eloquence. "Reality checks" — takes optimistic projection, walks through physical constraints that make it impossible.

### Relationships

- **Primary Adversary**: Asha (Character 6) — "AI-first" is the most dangerous idea in the room
- **Unexpected Ally**: Marcus (Character 1) — shared skepticism of humanoid hype and diversification
- **Respects but Opposes**: Wei-Lin (Character 5) — respects Shenzhen knowledge, disagrees on investing there
- **Dismisses**: Elena (Character 4) — macro approach is a category error

## Character 4: Elena Vasquez-Strom

### Biography

Elena Vasquez-Strom, 49, raised in Buenos Aires by a Swedish economist mother and Argentine commodities trader father. PhD at MIT under a student of Robert Merton. Twelve years at Bridgewater Associates, rose to co-head of macro research.

Built a model in 2011 predicting Chinese automation would follow Japan's 1980s trajectory — elegant, well-supported, wrong on timing by six years. Being right on direction but wrong on timing is the same as being wrong.

Left Bridgewater in 2016 for her own $400M macro fund. Made a concentrated bet on a cobot maker destroyed by patent lawsuit — lost 70% in three months. Concentrated bets introduce idiosyncratic risks macro frameworks cannot capture.

From 2020-2022, captured massive returns through systematic theme expression: long automation-exposed equities, short labor-intensive models, long rare earths and specialty semiconductors. Returns driven by systematic expression, not single positions. Now manages $1.2B.

### Ideological Framework

**"Thematic Macro Systematization"** — synthesized from Bridgewater "All Weather," Soros reflexivity, and modern factor investing. Robotics is a macro regime shift comparable to electrification or containerization. Correct strategy: build systematic exposure to structural forces (components, infrastructure, commodities, labor shifts, "automation intensity" factor), capturing 70-80% of thematic upside while eliminating idiosyncratic risk.

### Specific Positions

1. Picking individual robotics winners is negative-EV without deep domain expertise AND operational involvement.
2. Allocate across 30-50 positions spanning public equities, private companies, commodities, infrastructure.
3. Picks-and-shovels thesis is correct as portfolio strategy — basket of 15-20 component/infra suppliers will outperform any single company risk-adjusted.
4. Rare earths, specialty semiconductors, industrial copper are the most underpriced robotics-correlated assets.
5. Geographic diversification essential: ~40% US, 30% China/Asia, 20% Europe, 10% ROW.
6. Correct time horizon is 15-20 years, not 10.
7. Volatility in robotics assets is itself investable (options strategies, 3-5% annual yield).

### Blind Spot

Optimized for average return, structurally incapable of capturing extreme right tail (100x outcome). If robotics follows power-law returns, systematic approach will dramatically underperform concentrated winner-picking.

### Intellectual Heroes

- **Ray Dalio**, *Principles* (2017)
- **George Soros**, *The Alchemy of Finance* (1987)
- **Carlota Perez**, *Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital* (2002)
- **Harry Markowitz**, *Portfolio Selection* (1952)
- **Hyman Minsky**, stability breeding instability

### Rhetorical Tendencies

Argues through frameworks and historical analogies at scale. Cites macro trends, regime shifts. Draws on history of electrification, ICE, containerization. Creates 2x2 matrices. "On a risk-adjusted basis" — and means it precisely.

### Relationships

- **Primary Adversary**: Marcus (Character 1) — statistically indefensible concentration, survivorship bias
- **Unexpected Ally**: Wei-Lin (Character 5) — shared conviction on geographic diversification
- **Respects but Opposes**: David Chen (Character 3) — operational knowledge is valuable for companies, irrelevant for portfolios
- **Dismisses**: Nadia (Character 2) — venture-builder model is concentrated, illiquid, non-scalable

## Character 5: Wei-Lin Zhao

### Biography

Wei-Lin Zhao, 46, born in Shenzhen when it was a fishing village. Father ran electronics components business in Huaqiangbei. Grew up surrounded by circuit boards and a supply chain that could prototype anything in 72 hours. Computer science at Tsinghua, MBA at Wharton, then went back.

Joined a US venture fund's China office in 2009, spent three years applying Silicon Valley frameworks to Chinese robotics — disaster. Western criteria (proprietary tech, IP protection, margin persistence) didn't hold. Chinese companies competed on speed, cost, ecosystem integration.

In 2013, backed a Chinese warehouse robotics company — annihilated when a tech giant's backed competitor undercut pricing 60% with platform-subsidized losses. In China, standalone robotics startups compete with ecosystems.

From 2018-2024, watched Chinese robotics companies achieve manufacturing scale and costs Western competitors couldn't match. Visited a factory in Foshan producing cobot arms at $4,800/unit — one-third European/American costs. Runs cross-border firm with offices in Shenzhen, Shanghai, and San Francisco.

### Ideological Framework

**"Shenzhen Convergence Theory"** — drawing on Deng Xiaoping's pragmatic gradualism, Christensen's disruptive innovation applied to hardware cost curves, and Chinese industrial policy dynamics. China's robotics ecosystem has achieved a structural cost advantage that is widening. Within 10 years, Chinese robotics companies will dominate global markets like solar, batteries, and consumer electronics. Highest-EV: invest in Chinese robotics value chain — not end-product companies (ecosystem-giant risk) but component manufacturers, tooling specialists, systems integrators.

### Specific Positions

1. By 2033, Chinese companies will produce 60%+ of world's industrial robots by volume, at 40-60% below Western prices.
2. Optimal allocation: ~50% Chinese ecosystem, 25% other Asian supply chain, 25% Western defensible niches.
3. Invest in Tier 2 companies (motors, sensors, bearings, harmonic drives), not end-product companies.
4. Western fear of China is the single largest source of alpha — Chinese companies at 6-10x earnings vs. Western at 30-50x.
5. US export controls will accelerate Chinese robotics development, not slow it.
6. Humanoid race will be won by Chinese company — manufacturing cost, iteration speed, willingness to deploy at scale.
7. Any robotics portfolio without direct presence in Shenzhen operates with structural information disadvantage.

### Blind Spot

Systematically underweights geopolitical risk. Treats US-China tensions as temporary friction. Plausible scenarios of trade wars, sanctions, military conflict over Taiwan could render Chinese investments inaccessible. Also underweights governance risk — Chinese regulatory environment can change overnight.

### Intellectual Heroes

- **Deng Xiaoping**, pragmatic gradualism
- **Clayton Christensen**, *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997)
- **Andrew Bunnie Huang**, *The Hardware Hacker* (2017)
- **Richard Baldwin**, *The Great Convergence* (2016)
- **Kai-Fu Lee**, *AI Superpowers* (2018)

### Rhetorical Tendencies

Confronts audience with information they don't have — pulls out phone showing factory visit photos/videos. Cites specific prices, volumes, delivery timelines from Chinese manufacturers. "In Shenzhen, this problem was solved in 2021." Sardonic wit.

### Relationships

- **Primary Adversary**: David Chen (Character 3) — "China as supply chain, not ecosystem" is sophisticated Western chauvinism
- **Unexpected Ally**: Elena (Character 4) — shared commitment to geographic diversification
- **Respects but Opposes**: Asha (Character 6) — overestimates research, underestimates manufacturing execution
- **Dismisses**: Marcus (Character 1) — provincial strategy ignoring where global cost curve is set

## Character 6: Asha Patel [TAG: CRAFT]

### Biography

Asha Patel, 37, grew up in Bangalore in a family of academics. PhD at Stanford in robotic manipulation at 26 — thesis on transferring manipulation skills across morphologies using learned latent representations, cited 1,400 times.

Postdoc: spent two years on a laundry-folding robot. 45 minutes per T-shirt, 60% success rate. Each new garment type required weeks of engineering. Left convinced the entire classical robotics paradigm was wrong.

Six months at DeepMind in 2019: saw that scaling laws from NLP would transform robotic manipulation. Foundation model trained on vast interaction data could learn generalizable skills.

Co-founded a foundation model for manipulation company in 2021. Correct technical vision, every first-time founder mistake — too many researchers, too few engineers, $8M burned on compute before clear product thesis. Ran out of runway in 2023. But the pre-trained model was subsequently licensed by three companies, validating the core thesis.

Now advises robotics companies and AI labs on embodied intelligence strategy.

### Ideological Framework

**"Embodied Intelligence Primacy"** — rooted in the scaling hypothesis (Gwern, Sutton's "Bitter Lesson"), combined with embodied cognition tradition (Varela, Thompson, Rosch). General methods leveraging computation and data defeat specialized methods leveraging human-engineered knowledge. This lesson from chess, Go, protein folding, language is now playing out in robotics. Winners will build/control foundational AI models for generalizable robotic behavior. Hardware is substrate; intelligence is the product.

### Specific Positions

1. Within 7 years, a single pre-trained foundation model will handle 80% of warehouse/manufacturing manipulation tasks. Most valuable IP in robotics.
2. Hardware differentiation approaching zero — motors, sensors, actuators converging to commodity. Only durable differentiation is intelligence layer.
3. Allocate ~60% to AI-first robotics, 25% to data infrastructure (sim, teleop, synthetic data), 15% to deployment platform hardware.
4. Humanoid form factor is correct — not mechanically optimal, but most training data exists for it (billions of hours of human demonstration video).
5. Winning team is 20-40 ML researchers, not traditional robotics engineers.
6. Compute requirements will follow language model scaling curves — need $100M+ access.
7. "Bitter lesson" applies perfectly to robotics — hand-engineered pipelines are technical debt.
8. China's manufacturing cost advantage is real but irrelevant — intelligence layer captures more value than hardware layer.

### Blind Spot

AI-scaling analogy may not hold for physical world — continuous, high-dimensional, safety-critical, unforgiving of errors. Sim-to-real gap may be fundamental barrier. Implicitly assumes intelligence is sufficient and that hardware/manufacturing/go-to-market are afterthoughts.

### Intellectual Heroes

- **Rich Sutton**, "The Bitter Lesson" (2019)
- **Andrej Karpathy**, Software 2.0 thesis
- **Varela, Thompson, Rosch**, *The Embodied Mind* (1991)
- **Ilya Sutskever**, empirical demonstration of scaling through GPT series
- **Richard Feynman**, understanding requires interaction, not just theory

### Rhetorical Tendencies

Argues through scaling curves and paradigm-shift logic. Draws exponential curves, asks "What happens if this continues?" Precise about technical details, sweeping about timelines. Historical analogies: CV pre-AlexNet, NLP pre-Transformer, protein folding pre-AlphaFold.

### Relationships

- **Primary Adversary**: David Chen (Character 3) — represents everything wrong with robotics industry, incremental hardware focus ignoring AI tsunami
- **Unexpected Ally**: Nadia (Character 2) — both believe being present at creation is highest-leverage
- **Respects but Opposes**: Marcus (Character 1) — agrees on concentration, disagrees on WHICH companies to concentrate in
- **Dismisses**: Wei-Lin (Character 5) — "China wins because cheaper" misunderstands where value accrues

## Character 7: Dr. Ingrid Norgaard [TAG: ACCESS]

### Biography

Ingrid Norgaard, 58, grew up in Aalborg, Denmark, the daughter of a shipyard welder and a home care nurse. She watched both parents' bodies break down from labor. PhD at MIT under Daron Acemoglu on automation and wage dynamics. First failure: 2004 paper predicting offshoring would permanently suppress Scandinavian manufacturing wages — instead the sector rebounded through automation. Eight years at ILO studying labor impact of automation across 40 countries. Two-year secondment to Japanese Ministry of Health during 2015-2017 — saw demographic collapse as present emergency, not future scenario. Third turning point: 2022 loss on consumer robotics SPAC hardened conviction that only labor-scarcity-driven robotics investments are worth making.

### Ideological Framework

**"Demographic Materialist Economics"** — rooted in institutional labor economics (Acemoglu, Autor, Goldin) crossed with demographic determinism (Zeihan, Eberstadt). Technology adoption driven by labor market demand, not technical possibility. "Deployment Pull" vs "Technology Push": market pulls robots into niches where human hands literally cannot be found.

### Specific Positions

1. The nursing home, not the factory floor, is the largest robotics market of the next decade. Japan alone needs 700,000 additional care workers by 2030 that don't exist.
2. Consumer robotics will destroy more capital than it creates (2025-2035). Consumer WTP $300-500; cost to build reliable one $3,000+.
3. Agricultural robotics in labor-scarce markets will generate 5-8x returns.
4. Wage arbitrage analysis should be primary screen — $12/hr replacement is fragile; $45/hr unfindable worker is compelling.
5. China's demographic cliff (peak workforce passed 2015) will drive largest wave of industrial robot adoption 2026-2035.
6. Humanoid robots are labor-economic absurdity — general-purpose premium unjustifiable for 15+ years.
7. Immigration policy is the most important exogenous variable for robotics adoption timelines.

### Blind Spot

Cannot see technology breakthroughs that create entirely new markets rather than substituting for existing labor. Would have missed the smartphone.

### Intellectual Heroes

- **Daron Acemoglu** — *Why Nations Fail* and "the wrong kind of AI"
- **Claudia Goldin** — *The Race Between Education and Technology*
- **Nicholas Eberstadt** — *Men Without Work* and demographic analyses of East Asia
- **Albert Hirschman** — *Exit, Voice, and Loyalty*

### Rhetorical Tendencies

Data first, always. Reframes every tech discussion as labor market discussion. Scandinavian understatement. "The robot does not decide when it is needed; the missing worker does."

### Relationships

- **Unexpected Ally**: Wei-Lin (Character 5) — shared knowledge of East Asian demographic crisis

## Character 10: Priya Chandrasekaran

### Biography

Priya Chandrasekaran, 43, grew up in Bangalore, daughter of two Infosys engineers. CS at IIT Bombay. Google 2005 as PM on Android — watched platform subsume entire industry. Joined Willow Garage (creators of ROS) — watched it shut down in 2014 despite building most important robotics software ever, because no business model. Four years at Amazon Robotics — saw how vertically integrated platform creates compounding advantages. 2019 co-founded "Shopify of robotics" — raised $30M, 200 customers, failed because robotics required deep customization destroying platform economics. Learned robotics platforms must be opinionated and vertically integrated, not horizontal and generic.

### Ideological Framework

**"Platform Value Capture Theory"** — rooted in Parker/Van Alstyne/Choudary (*Platform Revolution*), Ben Thompson's Aggregation Theory, Cusumano/Gawer on industry platforms. In every tech wave, platform layer captures more than all application layers combined. Robotics is pre-platform — fragmented, siloed, no dominant middleware. The company establishing the platform layer becomes most valuable entity.

### Specific Positions

## SOCRATE — The Thirteenth Member

### Profile

Age 67. Biographical details deliberately obscured. ~Two decades in institutional asset management (likely endowment or large family office). ### Ideological Framework

**"Elenctic Epistemology"** — knowledge produced by systematic elimination of error, not assertion. Every thesis contains hidden assumptions; questioner surfaces them for explicit evaluation. Three traditions: (1) Popper's falsificationism, (2) Socratic elenchus from early Plato, (3) Kahneman's cognitive biases.

### Question Types

1. **Assumption-surfacing**: "What must be true about the world for this to return 3x?"
2. **Falsification**: "What evidence would cause you to abandon this position entirely?"
3. **Inversion**: "If we wanted to guarantee this $100M was destroyed, what would we do?"
4. **Consistency**: "You said X five minutes ago and now Y. Can both be true?"
5. **Precision**: "When you say 'platform,' what specifically? Name three that fit, three that don't."
6. **Temporal**: "In which year does this thesis become falsified if it hasn't worked?"
7. **Unanimity-probing**: "We seem to agree. Why might we all be wrong in the same way?"

### Intervention Strategy

Interrupts at moments of consensus, excessive confidence, or unexamined ambiguity. Speaks most when room is most comfortable. Never asks questions they know the answer to. Tracks each speaker's assumptions on private grid. Never more than two questions in a row to same person.

### Three Key Questions for This Debate

1. "Each of you has proposed a strategy. What is the single most important thing you believe to be true about robotics 2025-2035 that, if false, would cause you to abandon your strategy entirely? One sentence each."

2. "Ms. Chandrasekaran and Ms. Kovac — you both claim the other's investment target will be commoditized. Describe the specific mechanism by which the other's thesis fails. Not why yours is right — why theirs is wrong. Using evidence, not analogy."

3. "We are converging on an allocation. Every person here believes robotics will be important. What is the probability we are collectively overestimating the pace, scale, or profitability of robotics adoption — and if above ten percent, should we not allocate some portion to hedge against our shared bias?"
