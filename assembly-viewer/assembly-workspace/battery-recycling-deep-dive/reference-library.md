# Reference Library — Battery Recycling Deep Dive

## LAYER 1: INTELLECTUAL TRADITIONS

### Thermodynamic Realism — Kenji Tanaka
- **Josiah Willard Gibbs**, *On the Equilibrium of Heterogeneous Substances* (1876). The foundation for understanding that separating mixed substances requires energy proportional to the entropy of mixing. Kenji uses this to argue that multi-metal cathode recycling faces non-negotiable energy floors.
- **Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen**, *The Entropy Law and the Economic Process* (1971). Argued that economic processes are fundamentally constrained by thermodynamics — you cannot recycle indefinitely without energy inputs and material degradation. Kenji draws on this to challenge perpetual "closed loop" narratives.
- **Vaclav Smil**, *Energy and Civilization* (2017). The discipline of quantitative energy analysis applied to civilizational processes. Smil's insistence on "show me the numbers" is Kenji's methodological backbone.
- **Robert Ayres & Leslie Ayres**, *A Handbook of Industrial Ecology* (2002). Industrial metabolism as thermodynamic accounting. The framework for tracking energy and material flows through recycling processes honestly.

### Process Chemistry Perfectionism — Adele Fontaine
- **Glenn Seaborg**, actinide separation chemistry research (1940s-1970s). The intellectual foundation for solvent extraction and selective precipitation — techniques directly applicable to battery metal separation.
- **George Keller**, *Separation Process Engineering* (multiple editions). The engineering textbook that treats separation as an exact science with predictable yields, not an art.
- **Jean-Paul Glatz** and CEA reprocessing research. Adapting nuclear fuel cycle separation techniques (PUREX, TRUEX) to non-nuclear applications, including battery hydrometallurgy.
- **Terry Todd & IAEA**, advances in solvent extraction for spent fuel reprocessing. The industrial-scale proof that 99%+ selective separation is achievable at scale.

### Distributed Resource Sovereignty — Clara Mwangi
- **E.F. Schumacher**, *Small Is Beautiful* (1973). The case for appropriate technology scaled to local contexts, not imported industrial solutions.
- **Raúl Prebisch**, ECLAC dependency theory (1950s). Why commodity-exporting nations remain economically dependent — directly applicable to cobalt/lithium-exporting nations.
- **Daron Acemoglu & James Robinson**, *Why Nations Fail* (2012). Institutional explanation for resource curse dynamics in mining-dependent economies.
- **Ha-Joon Chang**, *Kicking Away the Ladder* (2002). How industrial policy built developed nations' manufacturing capacity — the blueprint for resource-sovereign industrialization.

### Market Mechanism Realism — Marcus Holloway
- **George Stigler**, *The Theory of Price*. Price theory as the fundamental analytical tool for understanding any market, including waste markets.
- **Michael Porter**, *Competitive Advantage* (1985). Value chain analysis applied to recycling — where does value accrue and who captures it?
- **Adam Minter**, *Junkyard Planet* (2013). The definitive account of how global recycling actually works — through markets, arbitrage, and entrepreneurs, not through policy mandates.
- **Daron Acemoglu**, work on induced technological change. When and why industries adopt new processes — the conditions under which recycling technology gets adopted vs. ignored.

### Full-Cost Comparative Analysis — Nikolai Petrov
- **Herman Daly**, *Steady-State Economics* (1977). The intellectual case for incorporating environmental costs into economic calculations.
- **Robert Costanza et al.**, "The Value of the World's Ecosystem Services and Natural Capital" (*Nature*, 1997). The landmark attempt to price ecosystem services — Nikolai's template for pricing mining externalities.
- **Gavin Mudd**, research on mining energy and water intensity at Monash University. The most rigorous quantitative analysis of mining's true resource costs, essential for honest mining-vs-recycling comparisons.
- **Guillaume Pitron**, *The Rare Metals War* (2020). The geopolitical framing of critical minerals dependency — why supply chain risk has economic value.

### Vertical Integration Manufacturing — Wei Zhang
- **Taiichi Ohno**, *Toyota Production System* (1988). Lean manufacturing and waste elimination as organizing principles — applied to the argument that standalone recycling adds unnecessary process steps.
- **Clayton Christensen**, *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997). Integrated architectures win when product performance isn't good enough — applied to why vertically integrated recycling outperforms modular approaches in a still-immature industry.
- **Barry Naughton**, *The Chinese Economy: Adaptation and Growth* (2018). Understanding China's industrial policy model — why CATL/Brunp's structure is a product of China's specific political economy.
- **Yet-Ming Chiang**, MIT battery materials research. The scientific bridge between materials discovery and manufacturing at scale — the intellectual tradition of treating battery production as a manufacturing engineering problem.

### Cross-Reading Assignments
- **Kenji** must engage: Christensen's *Innovator's Dilemma* — his thermodynamic analysis assumes static processes, but Christensen shows how integrated manufacturing systems can achieve performance improvements his models don't predict
- **Adele** must engage: Minter's *Junkyard Planet* — her process perfectionism needs to confront the reality that most recycling industries succeed through pragmatic, messy operations, not chemical elegance
- **Clara** must engage: Keller's *Separation Process Engineering* — to understand why some processes genuinely cannot be miniaturized without losing efficiency
- **Marcus** must engage: Costanza's ecosystem services valuation — his market prices omit real costs that will eventually be priced in through regulation or crisis
- **Nikolai** must engage: Ohno's *Toyota Production System* — to understand how manufacturing integration creates cost reductions that don't appear in his externality-adjusted cost curves
- **Wei** must engage: Acemoglu & Robinson's *Why Nations Fail* — to understand why China's vertically integrated model may not be replicable in different institutional environments

## LAYER 2: EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE

### What is the current state of battery recycling economics?
- Effective gross value per tonne of battery input: $4,200-$5,500 (2023-2033 projection, declining from $5,100 in 2023 to $4,200 in 2025). Source: Market analyses, 2024-2025. Relevant to: supports Kenji's and Marcus's skepticism about recycling margins.
- NMC batteries contain ~$8,700 of recoverable metal value per tonne of cells; LFP batteries contain ~$3,170. Source: Industry analyses, 2024. Relevant to: confirms Marcus's position that LFP recycling economics are structurally worse.
- Pyrometallurgical processing costs: $5-10/kg; Hydrometallurgical: $3-8/kg. Source: Industry benchmarks, 2024-2025. Relevant to: supports Adele's argument for hydrometallurgy on cost grounds.
- Recycling costs projected to decrease 40-60% by 2030 through technology and scale. Source: Industry forecasts, 2025. Relevant to: supports Wei's and Nikolai's optimism about cost curve trajectory.

### How do recovery rates compare across processes?
- Pyrometallurgy: 95%+ for Co and Ni, but only 30-50% for Li. Source: Multiple industry studies, 2024-2025. Relevant to: confirms Kenji's point about process trade-offs — you can't optimize all metals simultaneously.
- Hydrometallurgy: potentially >95% for Co, Ni, AND Li, but at higher process complexity and chemical waste. Source: Research reviews, 2024-2025. Relevant to: supports Adele's hydrometallurgy advocacy but confirms the waste issue Kenji raises.
- Hydrometallurgy shows 24.4% lower GHG emissions than pyrometallurgy. Source: 2025 lifecycle analysis (ScienceDirect). Relevant to: supports Nikolai's full-cost framework — even on carbon alone, hydro wins.

### What is the regulatory landscape?
- EU Battery Regulation (2023/1542): By 2031, mandatory 16% recycled cobalt and 6% recycled lithium content in new batteries. By 2027, 90% recovery targets for Co, Cu, Pb, Ni; 50% for Li. By 2031: 95% for Co/Cu/Pb/Ni, 80% for Li. Source: EUR-Lex, 2023. Relevant to: supports Marcus's view that gate fees will be necessary, supports Wei's view that this forces vertical integration.
- Carbon footprint declaration required from February 2025, labelling from 2026, QR code from 2027. Source: EU Battery Regulation implementation timeline. Relevant to: supports Nikolai's full-cost accounting framework — regulation is making externalities visible.
- IRA provides $45B+ in subsidies for domestic battery supply chains including recycling. Source: US policy, 2022-2025. Relevant to: supports Nikolai's argument that geopolitical premiums create a floor under recycling economics independent of commodity prices.

### What is the current industry capacity and structure?
- CATL/Brunp: expanded Foshan capacity to 120,000 tonnes in October 2025, including LFP line. Source: Industry reports, 2025. Relevant to: existence proof for Wei's vertical integration thesis.
- Redwood Materials: targeting 100 GWh capacity by 2025, 20,000 tonnes cathode powder by 2026. Received $1B+ investment. Source: Company reports, 2024-2025. Relevant to: supports Marcus's view that Redwood's cathode-supply-chain model is the right Western strategy.
- Li-Cycle: Glencore JV for 10,000 tonnes black mass annually from mid-2026; $475M DOE loan for New York plant. Source: Industry reports, 2025. Relevant to: shows the struggle of standalone recyclers (delays, financial difficulties) that Marcus predicts.
- Market projected to reach $114.66 billion by 2035. Source: Precedence Research, 2025. Relevant to: scale of opportunity, though Kenji would note market projections are not thermodynamic analyses.

### What is the state of direct recycling research?
- Single-step electrochemical process (Illinois Grainger, 2025) reduces recycling costs to 1/8th and environmental impact by 50%+ vs. conventional. Source: TechXplore, 2025. Relevant to: could challenge Kenji's thermodynamic skepticism if scalable.
- Upcycling NMC 111 to single-crystal NMC 622 via nickel ion diffusion demonstrated. Source: ACS Sustainable Chemistry & Engineering, 2025. Relevant to: addresses Kenji's objection about chemistry evolution — upcycling allows adaptation.
- Atilium/Imperial College London: direct regeneration of cathode materials with performance matching or exceeding virgin. Source: UK research, 2025. Relevant to: supports Adele's process chemistry approach if it can maintain quality at scale.
- Deep eutectic solvent approach for mixed LFP/Mn-rich cathode upcycling demonstrated. Source: Nature Communications, 2024. Relevant to: could make LFP recycling more viable, challenging Marcus's economics objection.

### What is the LFP recycling challenge?
- LFP batteries lack high-value Co and Ni — lithium is the only economically significant recoverable element. Source: Multiple studies, 2024-2025. Relevant to: fundamental economics challenge for all frameworks.
- Recyclers currently favor NMC; end-of-life LFP sold at negligible value or disposed as hazardous waste. Source: Industry analyses, 2025. Relevant to: supports Marcus's triage argument and challenges the "recycle everything" narrative.
- LFP batteries projected to outnumber NMC in the coming decade. Source: Market analyses, 2025. Relevant to: the LFP recycling problem gets bigger, not smaller — urgent for all frameworks.
- CATL/Brunp claims profitable LFP recycling through vertical integration. Source: Company operations, 2025. Relevant to: supports Wei's thesis that integration solves LFP economics.

### What are the adjacent opportunities?
- Graphite recycling: synthetic anode graphite costs $8,000-15,000/tonne, 90%+ sourced from China. Source: Industry data, 2024. Relevant to: Nikolai's most underappreciated opportunity thesis.
- Second-life battery applications (stationary storage) extending useful life by 5-10 years before recycling. Source: Multiple studies, 2024-2025. Relevant to: Clara's hierarchy of reuse > recycle.
- Battery passport/digital traceability requirements creating new software and data businesses. Source: EU Battery Regulation, 2023. Relevant to: adjacent business opportunity that none of the characters focus on directly.
