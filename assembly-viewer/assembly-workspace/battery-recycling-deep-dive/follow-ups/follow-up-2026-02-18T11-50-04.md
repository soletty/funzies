**Marcus Holloway:** You're absolutely right, and this is one of the most important dynamics in the recycling business that outsiders misunderstand. Let me be precise about how this works.

The term of art is "gate fees" — the price that battery producers or waste holders pay the recycler to accept their material. And yes, for end-of-life EV batteries, the waste holder typically pays. This is not hypothetical — it's how the industry operates today and how the broader waste management industry has always operated for materials that are expensive to handle.

Here's the mechanics. An automaker has a warranty obligation and regulatory liability for their end-of-life battery packs. Under the EU Battery Regulation, the producer bears extended responsibility. In the US, state-level hazardous waste rules apply. The automaker cannot throw these packs in a dumpster. They need someone licensed to take them, transport them safely, and process them. That someone charges for the service.

Current gate fees in the US and Europe range from roughly $1,000 to $3,000 per tonne of battery pack, depending on chemistry, format, state of health, and how much pre-processing the waste holder has done. For large-format EV packs that need safe discharge and disassembly, you're at the higher end. For pre-shredded black mass with known chemistry, the fee drops significantly or can even flip — black mass with high cobalt content is a positive-value commodity.

This changes the economics fundamentally. In our synthesis, we framed collection cost as a negative on the recycler's ledger. But when the waste holder is paying you to take the material, that cost becomes revenue. A recycler receiving $1,500/tonne in gate fees plus recovering $5,000-8,000/tonne in metal value has a very different margin picture than one paying $800-1,200/tonne for collection and then hoping to recover enough metal value to cover it.

Here's the nuance though: gate fees are not guaranteed and they're not uniform. They depend on the regulatory pressure on the waste holder. Strong EPR regimes (EU, Japan, South Korea) create real liability, which creates real willingness to pay. In jurisdictions with weak enforcement, producers will sit on batteries in warehouses or find cheaper, less responsible disposal channels. I've seen auto dealerships in the US with hundreds of warranty-return battery packs stacked in back lots because nobody's forcing them to do anything and they don't want to pay for proper disposal.

The other critical variable: chemistry. High-cobalt NMC packs command lower gate fees — or even positive purchase prices — because the recovered metals are worth enough to make the recycler profitable on metal value alone. LFP packs command *higher* gate fees because the metal value is minimal. So the gate fee is essentially the market pricing in the gap between processing cost and recovery value.

The net effect on recycling economics is this: gate fees create a floor under the business model that's independent of commodity prices. When cobalt crashes, your metal revenue drops, but gate fees can rise because the material is less attractive to recyclers and waste holders compete for limited processing capacity. It's a natural hedge. This is exactly how the municipal solid waste industry works — Waste Management Inc. makes its money on tipping fees at the landfill, not on selling recovered materials. The battery recycling industry is converging toward the same model, and honestly, it should.

**Dr. Wei Zhang:** Marcus is correct on the mechanics, and I want to add the dimension he's less familiar with — how this works in China and within integrated operations.

At CATL/Brunp, the gate fee dynamic barely exists for our primary feedstock. Manufacturing scrap from CATL's own production lines doesn't carry a gate fee — it's an internal transfer. The material is essentially free input, and the only costs are processing. This is another structural advantage of vertical integration that standalone recyclers don't have.

But for the end-of-life stream that's growing in China — and it's growing fast because China deployed EVs earlier than Europe or the US — the dynamic is similar to what Marcus describes, with one important difference. Chinese regulation through the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology requires automakers to establish collection channels. The compliance pressure creates the same willingness to pay that EPR does in Europe.

Where I'd push back slightly on Marcus: gate fees are not just a "waste management" revenue line. They're also a competitive weapon. Brunp and GEM can afford to charge *lower* gate fees than Western recyclers — or even pay for feedstock — because our integrated economics work at lower per-unit margins. We make money on the cathode precursor output, not on the gate fee or the recovered metal individually. So when a European automaker is deciding where to send their end-of-life packs, the Chinese integrated recycler can undercut the European standalone recycler on gate fee while still making money. This is already happening with black mass exports to China, and it's exactly the supply chain dependency dynamic that the EU Battery Regulation is trying to prevent.

**Nikolai Petrov:** The gate fee dynamic reinforces something I've been arguing throughout this assembly. When you incorporate gate fees into the recycling economics, you're effectively seeing the market internalize an externality — the cost of responsible disposal that mining never pays. A lithium mine doesn't pay anyone to take its tailings liability off its books in the same transparent way. The mine externalizes that cost to future generations or local communities.

The practical implication for investment analysis: a recycling business with reliable gate fee revenue of $1,500-2,000/tonne has a fundamentally different risk profile than one dependent on metal prices. You can model the gate fee as quasi-regulated revenue — it's driven by regulatory compliance obligations that are tightening, not loosening. The EU Battery Regulation's escalating recovery targets through 2031 will increase the volume of material that *must* be recycled, which sustains the gate fee market even if it puts downward pressure on per-unit fees through competition.

For LFP specifically, this is the critical insight. LFP recycling doesn't close on metal value alone — we've established that. But if gate fees for LFP packs are $2,000-3,000/tonne *because* nobody wants to handle them and producers are legally obligated to dispose of them, and your lithium recovery revenue is $1,500-2,500/tonne, you have a viable business at $4,000-5,500/tonne total revenue against processing costs of $3,000-4,000/tonne. It's thin, but it works — and it works *because* of regulatory pressure, not despite it. This is exactly the market correction mechanism I've been describing.

**Dr. Kenji Tanaka:** I don't dispute anything Marcus or Nikolai said about the gate fee structure. They're right that this improves the recycler's economics. But I want to make sure we don't lose sight of what this means thermodynamically and economically in the full picture.

Gate fees don't change the energy cost of separation. They shift who pays for it. The entropy cost of unmixing those cathode metals is identical whether the recycler pays for collection or the automaker pays the recycler to take it. What gate fees do is redistribute the economic burden — they make the recycler's P&L look better by moving a cost from the recycler's expense line to the producer's expense line. That's accounting, not physics.

The question I'd want to ask is: where does the automaker's gate fee money come from? Ultimately, from the vehicle price, which means from the consumer. So gate fees are effectively a hidden recycling tax on battery purchasers. I'm not saying that's wrong — it may be the right policy. But we should be honest that this isn't "recycling paying for itself." It's society paying for recycling through product prices, mediated by regulation. That's a perfectly legitimate policy choice, but it's different from the narrative that recycling is an inherently profitable activity.

**Clara Mwangi:** Everything discussed so far assumes a regulatory environment that creates and enforces producer responsibility. This is the reality in the EU, Japan, and increasingly in the US and China. But I need to point out that this gate fee ecosystem does not exist in most of the world.

In Kenya, in Nigeria, in India, in Indonesia — there is no EPR framework for lithium-ion batteries that creates the compliance pressure Marcus is describing. When batteries reach end-of-life in these markets, there's no automaker paying gate fees to a licensed recycler. The batteries enter the informal waste stream or accumulate in storage because there's no economic mechanism — neither gate fees nor metal value — to drive proper handling.

This is why the gate fee discussion, while accurate for the OECD, actually reinforces the geographic inequality I keep raising. The regulatory infrastructure that makes gate fees possible is itself a form of institutional capacity that developed nations have and developing nations largely don't. When we say "gate fees solve the recycling economics problem," we're really saying "gate fees solve the recycling economics problem in jurisdictions rich enough to implement and enforce extended producer responsibility."

The 500,000 motorcycle batteries reaching end-of-life in East Africa over the next five years won't generate gate fees. They'll generate a waste crisis.

**Marcus Holloway:** Clara's right, and I should have been clearer — gate fees are a feature of regulated markets, full stop. My experience is US and European. The informal sector dynamics she's describing are a genuinely different problem, and the gate fee model doesn't transfer.

But within regulated markets, to directly answer your question: yes, gate fees fundamentally improve recycler economics, they create a commodity-price hedge, and they're getting stronger as EPR mandates tighten. If I were advising an investor today, I'd model the gate fee revenue line as the stable base and the metal recovery as the upside — exactly the inverse of how most recycling business plans are written.
